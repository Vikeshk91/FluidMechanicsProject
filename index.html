Introduction

Ethylene glycol is a colorless, odorless, sweet-tasting organic compound with the chemical formula C₂H₆O₂. It is a type of alcohol and is commonly used in a variety of industrial and household applications.

Physical Properties

Chemical Properties

  1. Functional Groups:

    2. Ethylene glycol contains two hydroxyl groups, making it a diol (a type of alcohol). This gives it the ability to participate in various chemical reactions typical of alcohols.

  2. Hydrogen Bonding:

    3. The presence of two hydroxyl groups allows ethylene glycol to form strong intermolecular and intramolecular hydrogen bonds, influencing its boiling point, solubility, and viscosity.

  3. Acidity and Basicity:

    4. Ethylene glycol is neutral in nature but exhibits weak acidic behavior due to the hydroxyl groups. It does not ionize significantly in water but can participate in proton-transfer Reactions.

  4. Oxidation:

    5. Ethylene glycol can be oxidized to produce aldehydes, ketones, or acids depending on the conditions.

  5. Esterification:

    6. Reacts with carboxylic acids or acid derivatives to form esters.

  6. Reaction with Acids:

    7. Ethylene glycol reacts with mineral acids like to undergo dehydration, leading to the formation of cyclic ethers such as dioxane.

  7. Reaction with Bases:

    8. It can react with strong bases to form glycolate salts. In the presence of alkali metal hydroxides, ethylene glycol can undergo elimination reactions under certain conditions.

Applications

  1. Antifreeze and Coolants

    2. Ethylene glycol is most commonly used as a coolant and antifreeze in automotive radiators, industrial cooling systems, and HVAC systems. It lowers the freezing point and raises the boiling point of water, preventing it from freezing in cold temperatures and boiling over in hot conditions.

  2. Hydraulic Fluids and Brake Fluids

    3. Ethylene glycol is used in hydraulic fluids and brake fluids for its ability to maintain stable viscosity over a wide range of temperatures and pressure conditions.

  3. Polymer Production

    4. Ethylene glycol is used in the production of plasticizers to enhance the flexibility and durability of plastics, especially PVC.

  4. Water Treatment

    5. Ethylene glycol can be used as a flocculant in water treatment processes to aid in the removal of impurities from wastewater and drinking water.

  5. Paints and Coatings

    6. Paint and Coating Solvent: Due to its ability to dissolve various substances, ethylene glycol is used as a solvent in the production of paints, varnishes, and coatings.

Process Design

Process Description

Ethylene glycol (C2H6O2) is industrially prepared via a two-step process involving the oxidation of ethylene to ethylene oxide and the subsequent hydrolysis of ethylene oxide. In the first step, ethylene is oxidised in a continuous stirred-tank reactor (CSTR) using molecular oxygen in the presence of a silver catalyst. The reaction typically operates at temperatures between 200°C and 300°C and pressures around 1–2 atm. The silver catalyst ensures high selectivity for ethylene oxide, although strict control of temperature and oxygen-to-ethylene ratios is crucial to minimise the formation of unwanted by-products like carbon dioxide and water.


The ethylene oxide produced in the first step is then fed into another reactor, often a CSTR, for hydrolysis. This step involves mixing ethylene oxide with water in a 1:10 molar ratio, ensuring a high water-to-ethylene oxide ratio to suppress the formation of diethylene glycol and triethylene glycol by-products. The hydrolysis reaction is exothermic and occurs efficiently without the need for a catalyst at moderate temperatures of 50°C to 70°C and pressures of 1–2 atm. The use of a CSTR in this stage facilitates uniform mixing and temperature control, which are essential for optimizing the reaction rate and product quality.


Following hydrolysis, the reaction mixture undergoes separation and purification. Fractional distillation is used to separate ethylene glycol from unreacted water and other minor by-products. Water and any unreacted ethylene oxide are recycled back into the system to enhance efficiency and yield. The purified ethylene glycol, which may require additional steps to meet specific quality standards, is then suitable for applications such as antifreeze formulations, polyester production, and industrial solvents.


This process, with its use of well-controlled reactors like CSTRs, ensures high product yield and scalability while maintaining safety and environmental standards. However, handling intermediates like ethylene oxide requires stringent safety measures due to their toxicity and flammability.

Handling & Storage Calculations

Storage and handling of ethylene glycol should be done with care to ensure safety due to its toxicity and potential hazards. Below are key guidelines for the proper storage and handling of ethylene glycol:

Storage Guidelines:

  1. Storage Location:

    2. Store ethylene glycol in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area.

    Keep containers away from sources of heat, direct sunlight, and open flames, as ethylene glycol is flammable under certain conditions.

    Secure containers to prevent accidental spills or damage.

  2. Container Requirements:

    3. Store in properly labeled, sealed containers to prevent leaks or contamination. Use containers made from materials that are compatible with ethylene glycol, such as plastic, steel, or glass.

    Avoid storing in aluminum containers, as ethylene glycol can react with aluminum under certain conditions.

  3. Temperature Control:

    4. Maintain the storage temperature within the manufacturer's recommended range, generally between 5°C to 35°C (41°F to 95°F). This helps prevent degradation and maintains the stability of ethylene glycol.

  4. Separation from Incompatible Substances:

    5. Keep ethylene glycol away from strong oxidizing agents, acids, and other reactive chemicals. These substances could cause dangerous reactions.

    Store it away from food, feed, and drinking water, as ingestion can be harmful or fatal.

Handling Guidelines:

  1. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):

    2. Always wear appropriate protective clothing, such as gloves, goggles, and a lab coat or chemical-resistant suit to prevent skin contact and exposure.

    If working in environments where vapor exposure is possible, use respiratory protection such as an approved mask or respirator.

    Eye wash stations and safety showers should be available in case of accidental exposure.

  2. Avoiding Ingestion and Exposure:

    3. Do not ingest ethylene glycol. Keep it away from food and drink sources.

    Ensure that drinking water and eating areas are clearly separated from areas where ethylene glycol is handled.

    Wash hands thoroughly after handling ethylene glycol to avoid accidental ingestion or contamination.

  3. Handling Leaks or Spills:

    4. In case of a spill, immediately contain the area to prevent spreading. Use absorbent materials like sand, vermiculite, or commercial spill pads to soak up the liquid.

    Clean up spills promptly using appropriate procedures, and dispose of the collected material according to local environmental and safety regulations.

    If the spill is significant, evacuate the area and call for emergency assistance.

  4. Disposal:

    5. Dispose of ethylene glycol and any contaminated materials in accordance with local hazardous waste disposal regulations. Do not pour it down the drain or dispose of it in regular trash.

    For large quantities, contact a licensed hazardous waste disposal contractor.

  5. Training:

    6. Ensure all employees or individuals handling ethylene glycol are properly trained in safe handling procedures, spill management, and emergency response actions.
